Other Information

This App is a tool to guide you through a risk assessment and response for any individual you may think is experiencing a mental health crisis.

The App is a support system to help you ask the right questions in a step-by-step process, and respond accordingly to the results based on the accuracy and truthfulness of the answers provided.

The App produces results from a compilation of currently validated measures and best practice modalities used in the mental health field.

The App does not provide a clinical diagnosis, nor does it justify the means to secure an involuntary psychiatric hospitalization.

The App is designed to bridge the gap between people in need of mental health services and the service providers already in place.

Using the Assessment

When using the assessment feature the "?" will provide you additional assistance:

"Ask another way" will appear for certain questions that may be difficult for the assessed to answer, this feature populates an alternative way of asking the question.

(*some questions have only one way of asking, because they need to be asked that way).

"Call 911" allows you to have the ability to contact emergency services immediately if you feel the individual's or your safety is in jeopardy.

"Exit Assessment" Allows you to leave the current assessment and delete the report.

"Return" Allows you to return to the current assessment.

  • Assessment Recommendations
    Tips for Assessing
    • Be kind and courteous towards the individual.
    • Do not make judgmental statements or remarks to any of the individual's responses.
      • Example: You shouldn't feel that way; don't look at it like that; it’s not really that bad, etc.
    • Do not feel the need to rush through the assessment.
      • Provide response time for the individual to share information.
    • Realize the response plan generated is a "good plan" but is not limited to the information provided. You may be able to create a personalized plan for that individual based on your relationship.
    • Do not jeopardize your or another’s safety to intervene and assess.
      • Get help when help is needed.
      • If you are concerned about someone’s wellbeing, do not leave the individual alone.
    • Reach out to someone else that knows the individual for additional support.
    • Remember, just because an individual engages in self-injurious behaviors or self-harm doesn’t always mean that individual is attempting suicide.
      • Increased risk of suicide is higher when the individual has thoughts of doing it, a plan to do it, access to a means to do it, and the individual does not feel supported by family and friends. Other factors such as a history of abuse and being bullied can also increase the chance of suicide.
    • Realize the results in these reports are generalized and not 100% accurate.
    • Above all else, TRUST YOURSELF!
      • Listen to your instincts and be yourself. Empathy and compassion for the individual’s situation will show them that you care.
  • Know the Signs
    • Appearing overly depressed.
    • Increased use of drugs or alcohol.
    • Consistent troubles sleeping and eating.
    • Giving away their prized Possessions.
    • Isolating themselves from friends and family.
    • Been a victim of bullying.
    • Extended irritability, anxiety and or rage.
    • Loss of interest.
    • Significant difficulties returning from military service.
    • Feelings of being trapped and living with unbearable pain.
    • Making statements of wanting to die.
    • History of harming themselves.
    • Recent Loss (family member, friend, relationship/partner, job, etc.).
    • History of traumatic experiences.
    These "signs" are guidelines for understanding how an individual may be at a higher risk of self-harm/suicide.

    A single factor, or a combination of these signs can occur.

    It is also important to understand the access to lethal means that an individual may have if they are experiencing a crisis.
    • For example: owning or the ability to easily get a gun, access to drugs and medications, razors, etc.
      • Reducing access in a state of crisis is always the right support.
